COMPANY LAW

How to register a company with SECP: a step-by-step overview

Incorporating a company in Pakistan is handled by the Securities and Exchange Commission of Pakistan (SECP), and the entire process now runs online through SECP's eZfile portal. Most applicants choose between a single member (private) company, a private limited company or — less commonly — a public limited company. The right choice depends on the number of participants, financing plans and governance needs.

The first formal step is name reservation. You propose a company name through the portal, which is checked against existing names and the restrictions in the Companies Act, 2017 — names that are identical or deceptively similar to existing companies, or that contain prohibited or sensitive words without permission, will be refused. A successful reservation is typically valid for sixty days, within which the incorporation application must follow.

Next comes the incorporation application itself. The key documents are the Memorandum of Association, which states the company's principal line of business, and the Articles of Association, which set out its internal rules. You will also provide particulars and identity documents of subscribers and directors, a registered office address, and details of authorised and paid-up capital. The filing fee varies with authorised capital and is lower for online submission. Once SECP is satisfied, it issues a certificate of incorporation — for straightforward online applications this often takes only a few working days.

Incorporation is the beginning, not the end. A new company should promptly attend to post-incorporation registrations: obtaining its National Tax Number with the Federal Board of Revenue, opening a company bank account, registering for sales tax where its activities require it (including provincial registrations for services), and registering with employee welfare institutions once staff are hired. The Companies Act also imposes continuing obligations — maintaining statutory registers, filing annual returns and financial statements, and notifying SECP of changes in directors, address or capital. Missing these filings attracts penalties, so a compliance calendar from day one is a sound investment.

COMPLIANCE

Understanding AML/CFT compliance obligations in Pakistan

Pakistan's anti-money-laundering and counter-terrorist-financing regime is anchored in the Anti-Money Laundering Act, 2010, as amended, and is aligned with the international standards of the Financial Action Task Force (FATF). At the centre of the system sits the Financial Monitoring Unit (FMU), which receives and analyses reports from the regulated sector, while sectoral regulators — principally the State Bank of Pakistan for banks and exchange companies and the SECP for non-bank financial companies, insurers and brokers — supervise compliance. Designated non-financial businesses and professions, such as real estate agents, jewellers, lawyers and accountants, are also brought within the net through their own supervisory arrangements.

The core obligations follow a risk-based approach. A reporting entity must identify and verify its customers before entering a relationship — the familiar know-your-customer and customer due diligence process — and must look through corporate structures to identify beneficial owners. Higher-risk relationships, including politically exposed persons, call for enhanced due diligence and senior-management approval. Customers and transactions must be screened against the proscribed-persons and United Nations sanctions lists, and assets of designated persons must be frozen without delay under the targeted financial sanctions framework.

Two reporting duties matter most in practice: suspicious transaction reports, which must be filed with the FMU promptly whenever there are reasonable grounds to suspect that funds are linked to crime, and currency transaction reports for cash transactions above the prescribed threshold. Records of transactions and identification data must be retained for at least five years. An effective program also requires a designated compliance officer, ongoing employee training and independent testing of controls.

The consequences of failure are serious: administrative penalties, regulatory enforcement affecting licences, and in appropriate cases criminal liability for the entity and responsible officers. Businesses that treat AML/CFT as a governance priority — rather than a paperwork exercise — are far better placed when the regulator calls.

VIRTUAL ASSETS

Virtual assets regulation in Pakistan: what businesses should know

For years, the position of virtual assets in Pakistan was defined by caution. In 2018 the State Bank of Pakistan directed banks and other regulated entities not to deal in virtual currencies or facilitate their customers' crypto transactions, which kept the sector outside the formal financial system even as retail interest grew. Holding crypto was not criminalised, but participants had no regulatory protection and businesses had no licensing path.

That position has shifted decisively. In 2025 Pakistan moved to create a dedicated regulatory framework for the sector: virtual assets legislation established the Pakistan Virtual Assets Regulatory Authority (PVARA) as the licensing and supervisory body for virtual asset service providers, and government-level initiatives signalled an intention to bring exchanges, custodians and related businesses into a regulated perimeter. The framework is designed with FATF standards in mind — in particular Recommendation 15, which requires countries to license or register virtual asset service providers and subject them to full AML/CFT obligations.

For businesses, three practical points follow. First, licensing readiness: entities operating or planning virtual-asset services should prepare the governance, fit-and-proper, capital and systems documentation that a licensing application will demand. Second, AML/CFT capability is non-negotiable — customer due diligence, transaction monitoring, sanctions screening and arrangements for the FATF "travel rule" on transfer information should be designed into the platform, not bolted on. Third, tax and accounting treatment of digital assets remains an evolving area, and positions taken now should be documented and defensible.

The regulatory landscape is developing quickly, and the details of licensing windows, categories and conditions continue to take shape. Businesses in this sector should verify the current status of the framework before acting, and build compliance into their plans from the outset rather than retrofitting it after enforcement begins.
